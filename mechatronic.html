    Description

    The project involved the design and development of an Automated Guided Vehicle (AGV) in collaboration with Southern Cross Ceramics and RMIT University. Using technologies such as depth sensors and RGB cameras, the team created a smaller-scale prototype AGV capable of autonomous navigation, obstacle avoidance, and line following.

    The project aimed to address manufacturing industry challenges during the pandemic by providing a resilient and efficient solution. Through extensive research, design iterations, and problem-solving, the team successfully delivered a functional prototype, demonstrating the potential for future development in the factory environment. The collaboration between RMIT University and Southern Cross Ceramics showcased the integration of advanced technologies for industrial automation.

    Objectives and Processes

    The project had several objectives aimed at designing and developing an Automated Guided Vehicle (AGV) to address the challenges faced by the manufacturing industry, particularly during the global pandemic. The main objectives were to create an AGV that could navigate autonomously, avoid obstacles, follow a predefined path, and turn tight corners within the factory environment. Additionally, the project sought to develop a prototype that could demonstrate the feasibility of the AGV concept and serve as a foundation for potential future development.

    To achieve these objectives, the team followed a systematic process that encompassed research, planning, design, and implementation. The process began with a thorough exploration of existing AGV technologies and a review of relevant literature. This research phase helped the team gain a comprehensive understanding of the components, capabilities, and limitations of AGVs, as well as identify potential solutions for the project's requirements.

    Based on the research findings, the team formulated plans for the AGV design. Initially, Plan A involved constructing a full-sized AGV capable of pushing trolleys loaded with ceramic tiles. However, considering time limitations and the risk of incomplete development, the team decided to shift to Plan B, which involved creating a smaller-scale prototype AGV without load-bearing capabilities. This decision allowed the team to focus on demonstrating the key functionalities and proving the concept to Southern Cross Ceramics.

    The design process involved iterating and refining the AGV's mechanical and electrical components. The team utilized Computer-Aided Design (CAD) software to develop the prototype's design, ensuring the integration of sensors, motors, and control systems. Material selection was a crucial consideration, and the team opted for 3D printed PLA plastic due to its suitability for the prototype's smaller scale and absence of load requirements.

    Implementation of the design involved assembling the prototype AGV and integrating the necessary hardware and software components. The team followed a structured work schedule, assigning tasks and responsibilities to team members. Regular meetings with the course coordinator and project supervisor facilitated progress tracking and provided guidance throughout the implementation phase.

    Testing and fine-tuning were crucial stages of the project. The prototype AGV underwent rigorous testing to validate its autonomous navigation, obstacle avoidance, and line following capabilities. The team identified and addressed issues such as shadow disruption on the line-following track, time delays during direction changes, and software bugs. By implementing solutions and refining the design and code, the team ensured that the prototype performed as intended.

    Throughout the project, risk assessment was conducted to identify potential risks and mitigate them accordingly. The team maintained effective communication channels with Southern Cross Ceramics to align the project's progress with their expectations. Feedback from the industrial partner was continuously sought and incorporated into the design and development process.

    Results

    The project yielded significant results in terms of the design, development, and functionality of the Automated Guided Vehicle (AGV) prototype. Despite the shift from the original plan for a full-sized AGV to a smaller-scale prototype, the team successfully demonstrated key features and met the requirements set by Southern Cross Ceramics.

    The prototype AGV showcased autonomous navigation capabilities, accurately following the predefined path marked by black tape on the factory floor. The RGB camera installed on the AGV effectively detected and tracked the tape, enabling precise line following. This feature proved crucial in ensuring the AGV's ability to maneuver through the factory environment without deviating from its intended path.

    Obstacle avoidance was another vital functionality of the prototype AGV. Equipped with a REALSENSE D415 depth sensor, the AGV effectively detected and responded to obstacles in its path. The depth sensor provided a reliable and accurate reading, enabling the AGV to navigate around people and goods left on the factory floor, ensuring safety and preventing collisions.

    During testing, the AGV demonstrated its capability to turn tight corners, meeting the specific requirements of Southern Cross Ceramics. The design of the AGV, with separate motors for the rear wheels and a front wheel capable of 180-degree rotation, facilitated smooth maneuverability even in limited spaces.

    One of the notable outcomes of the project was the successful integration of various technologies and components into the AGV system. The team researched and selected appropriate sensors, including the REALSENSE D415 depth sensor and RGB camera, to ensure optimal performance in obstacle detection and line following. The implementation of these sensors, along with the integration of microcontrollers and motor control systems, resulted in a fully functional AGV prototype.

    The project also highlighted the importance of continuous improvement and problem-solving. Throughout the development process, the team encountered various challenges, such as shadow disruptions on the line-following track and time delays during direction changes. By applying solutions such as adjusting the tape color, changing sensor locations, implementing PID controllers, and updating software codes, the team effectively addressed these challenges and improved the AGV's performance.

    Furthermore, the prototype AGV showcased the feasibility and potential of using advanced automation technology in the manufacturing industry. It served as a proof of concept, demonstrating the benefits of implementing AGVs for tasks such as material transportation within a factory setting. The successful development and testing of the prototype provided valuable insights for Southern Cross Ceramics and laid the foundation for future advancements in AGV technology in collaboration with RMIT University.

    The project's outcomes also extended beyond the immediate objectives. The collaboration between RMIT University and Southern Cross Ceramics fostered a valuable partnership, facilitating knowledge exchange and industry-academia cooperation. The feedback received from Southern Cross Ceramics regarding the prototype was positive, indicating a high level of satisfaction with the project's results.

    Overall, the results of the project demonstrated the team's ability to design, develop, and deliver a functional AGV prototype that fulfilled the objectives and requirements set by Southern Cross Ceramics. The successful integration of technologies, autonomous navigation, obstacle avoidance, and line following capabilities showcased the potential for AGVs to enhance efficiency, productivity, and safety in the manufacturing industry. The project's outcomes provided a solid foundation for future development and collaboration, contributing to the advancement of automation and robotics in industrial settings.
